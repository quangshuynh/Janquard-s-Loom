Jacquard's Loom

Invented in 1804



Definition


The Janquard's Loom, created in 1804 by Joseph Janquard, was an advanced device that attached to power looms to tell the loom to form complex textiles. The significance of the Janquard's Loom was that it allowed unskilled weavers to design complicated weaves on textiles. Furthermore, the use of human-machine interaction to write binary using punch cards would be later developed into the modern computer. Moreover, the loom can control the warp threads individually, which meant it could create very advanced patterns in the clothes with better accuracy and speed.










Function


The significance of the Janquard's Loom had the basic concept of inserting punch cards to program the pattern of the weave. This was one of the first ideas that revolved around inputting data to automate a process. The holes in the punch card are read by the appartus and they tell the loom what pattern to create. Then, there are pins in the appartus that can read the punch cards. The holes tell the appartus which threads underneath should be raised for each pass. Eventually, the idea of punch cards made it to the first computers. Punch cards carried instructions for the calculations in the computer.
